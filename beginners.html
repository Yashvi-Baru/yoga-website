Yoga for Beginner's

1. Malasana (Garland Pose)

This beautiful squat is one of my all-time favorite poses. Malasana releases the lower back, opens the hips, and turns the practitioner into a cute little nugget. Explore variations and tips on how to make this pose easier or how to go deeper.

How to do -

  • Bend the knees and lower your butt toward the floor to come into a squat.
  • Take your upper arms inside your knees and bend the elbows to bring the palms together into prayer position.
  • Keep your spine straight, your butt moving toward the floor, and your shoulders relaxed away from your ears.

Benefits -

  • Stretches the ankles, groins and back torso
  • Tones the belly

2. Chaturanga Dandasana (Four-Limbed Staff Pose)

Chaturanga is one of the most common postures in Vinyasa yoga—but also one of the most abused. Students tend to rush this pose, cheating its alignment, which with repetition can lead to injury.

How to do -

  • Perform Adho Mukha Svanasana, then Plank Pose. Firm your shoulder blades against your back ribs
  • With an exhalation slowly lower your torso and legs to a few inches above and parallel to the floor.
  • Hold elbow in by the sides of the torso and push them back toward the heels.
  • Lift the top of the sternum and your head to look forward.

Benefits -

  • Strengthens the arms and wrists
  • Tones the abdomen

3. Utthita Trikonasana (Extended Triangle Pose)

Trikonasana. Such a classic standing pose! We live in a world where standing poses are often ignored, but this one is part of my regular practice come rain or shine. It is a glorious way to release your lower back, strengthen your core, and expand your body (and mind).

How to do -

  • Stand in Tadasana, With an exhalation, step or lightly jump your feet 3 1/2 to 4 feet apart.
  • Raise your arms parallel to the floor and shoulder blades wide, palms down.
  • Turn your left foot in slightly to the right and your right foot out to the right 90 degrees.
  • Turn your right thigh outward, extend your torso to the right directly over the plane of the right leg, bending from the hip joint
  • Rest your right hand on your shin, ankle, whatever is possible without distorting the sides of the torso.
  • Stretch your left arm toward the ceiling, in line with the tops of your shoulders.

    • Benefits -

    • Stretches and strengthens the thighs, knees, and ankles
    • Stretches the hips, groins, hamstrings, and calves; shoulders, chest, and spine
    • Stimulates the abdominal organs
    • Helps relieve stress
    • Improves digestion
    • Helps relieve the symptoms of menopause

    4 .Crescent Lunge

    I can’t imagine a yoga practice without this perfect standing pose. Crescent Lunge my go-to for opening my hips and psoas, encouraging space in my chest, and feeling powerful on my feet.

    How to do -

    • Start in Adho Mukha Svanasana , Exhale and step your right foot forward between your hands
    • Aligning your knee over the heel ,Keep your left leg strong and firm.
    • Sweep your arms wide to the sides and raise them overhead, palms facing.
    • Lengthen your tailbone toward the floor and reach back through your left heel , help support your chest, Look up toward your thumbs.

    Benefits -

    • Increases your stamina and balance
    • Improves your posture by awakening the spine and back muscles
    • Tones abdominal wall

    5. Parivrtta Utkatasana (Revolved Chair Pose)

    I know Chair Pose isn’t the most popular pose in the world, but this is why it’s on the list. It’s a great foundational pose to teach us how to twist safely. If you can learn the mechanics of a twist here, you’ll be safer in more advanced postures.

    How to do -

    • Stand in Tadasana, Inhale to lift your arms overhead,
    • Shift your weight into your heels and bend your knees, shifting your hips back and down into Utkatasana.
    • Bring your hands into Anjali Mudra in front of your heart
    • Keep your weight in left heel, Draw your left thighbone back to keep your hips and knees even.
    • Gently roll your belly, chest, neck, and head even more to the right, twisting deeper.

    Benefits -

    • Strengthens the thighs and ankles
    • Stretches the Achilles tendons and shins
    • Tones your digestive organs and heart.

    6. Salamba Sirsasana (Supported Headstand)

    Inversions are a magical group of postures that reverses our perspective and give us a strong dose of empowerment. Tripod Headstand is one of the easier inversions to balance because of the large foundation. It’s also fantastic to understand if you want to move into advanced transitions such as lowering into arm balances.

    How to do -

    • Place the crown of your head on the ground with your hands shoulder-width apart and elbows stacking over the heels of your hands.
    • Curl your toes under and straighten your legs to enter a Dolphin Pose.
    • Straight legs lift together until ankles, knees, hips and shoulders are stacked

    Benefits -

    • calm the mind.
    • stimulate the lymphatic system.
    • alleviate stress and depression.
    • strengthen the upper body, spine, and core.
    • stimulate and strengthen abdominal organs.

    7. Salamba Setu Bandha Sarvangasana (Supported Bridge Pose)

    This is my happy place. Funny enough, traditional Bridge Pose makes me crazy. Add a block under the lower back and voilà—I could stay there for hours with a smile on my face. This is a great modification for Shoulderstand and a relaxing way to release the front body and release the spine.

    How to do -

    • Lie on the floor,Bend your knees and set your feet on the floor, heels as close to the sitting bones as possible.
    • Pressing your inner feet and arms actively into the floor and lift the buttocks off the floor.
    • Lift your buttocks until the thighs are about parallel to the floor.

    Benefits -

    • Calms the brain and helps alleviate stress and mild depression
    • Helps relieve the symptoms of menopause
    • Reduces anxiety, fatigue, backache, headache, and insomnia
    • Stimulates abdominal organs, lungs, and thyroid
    • Stretches the chest, neck, and spine

    8. Ustrasana (Camel Pose)

    Camel is a love-hate pose for many people. The key is to trigger all of the proper alignment in the body to keep the lower back supported and the neck happy. The beauty lies in the fact that there are so many variations. So here we go!

    How to do -

    • Stand on your shins with your knees and feet hip-width apart.
    • Wrap your hands around your hips encouraging your tailbone to drop down while your lower belly lifts up to neutralize your pelvis.
    • Keep your hands on your hips and lift your heart up powerfully as you roll your shoulder heads back.
    • Hold here with hands on the hips for about 8 breaths.

    Benefits -

    • Can help build confidence and empowerment
    • May help relieve back pain
    • Strengthens your back muscles, back of your thighs, and buttocks
    • Improves posture and counteracts the effects of prolonged sitting and doing computer work

    9. Janu Sirsasana (Head-to-Knee Forward Bend)

    This one may seem random, but I have affection for it going all the way back to my Ashtanga days. This fabulous forward fold releases the calf and hamstring of the straight leg with the added benefit of opening the hip of the bent-knee leg. It also teaches the student to notice the effects of small nuances, such as squaring the chest with the straight-leg knee.

    How to do -

    • Place a strap over the ball of your straight-leg foot.
    • Bend your right knee,rest your right foot sole lightly against your inner left thigh
    • Reach out with your right hand to take the inner left foot, thumb on the sole.
    • Use the pressure of the left hand on the floor to increase the twist to the left. Then reach your left hand to the outside of the foot.

    Benefits -

    • Stimulates the liver and kidneys
    • Relieves anxiety, fatigue, headache, menstrual discomfort
    • Calms the brain and helps relieve mild depression
    • Improves digestion

    10. Viparita Karani (Legs-up-the-Wall Pose)

    This is such a glorious pose and great for all levels of students! Leg-Up-the-Wall Pose is the best way to relax after a long day or practice on your feet. It drains the legs and is also a fantastic posture if you struggle with insomnia.

    How to do -

    • Sit on it sideways with one hip touching the wall.
    • As you lie down, swivel around and sweep your legs up the wall, keeping your lower back elevated.

    Benefits -

    • Relieves tired or cramped legs and feet
    • Gently stretches the back legs, front torso, and the back of the neck
    • Relieves mild backache
    • Calms the mind
